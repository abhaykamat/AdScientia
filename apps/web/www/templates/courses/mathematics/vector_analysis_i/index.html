Vector Analysis I

Introduction

Why are we interested in studying calculus with vector quantities? It turns out that many areas of applied mathematics and physics make use of vector quantities to describe physical phenomena. For instance, the velocity of a fluid flowing through a river and the electromagnetic forces generated by a point charge can both be described via a vector field, a fundamental concept in vector analysis.

This second year course will build on the concepts defined in the first year courses Linear Algebra I, Analysis I and Analysis II. It will reintroduce the notion of a vector, scalar field and vector field. Then we will proceed to study elementary vector operations such as the scalar (dot) product, the vector (cross) product and the triple product using the matrix notation that we are familiar with from Linear Algebra.

After defining these elementary operations it is necessary to discuss differentiation in the context of more than one real variable. We will study spatial concepts such as paths, level sets and graphs. We will then present some fundamental results including Taylor's Theorem and the Chain Rule. We will then discuss partial differentiation and how it applies to functions of more than one real variable.

The next step will be to introduce the vector differential operators - gradient, divergence and curl. They will all be shown to have a ready physical significance. These tools will form the basic building blocks of partial differential equations which are used to describe the behaviour of multi-dimensional physical phenomena. We will also introduce some useful vector identities which will be indispensible in your later studies in Applied Mathematics.

Having discussed vector differentiation, we also need to spend time analysing how (and what) we can integrate in a vector context. We will discuss line, surface and volume integration as extensions of integration in a one-dimensional space.

At this stage we are ready to study some fundamental theorems of vector analysis, such as Green's, Stokes' and Gauss' Theorems. These will allow us to relate integration of functions on surface boundaries to integration of funtions on spaces contained in these boundaries. These theorems are indispensible in the study of electromagnetism, fluid dynamics and gravitation where conservative (zero curl) fields play a large part.

Up until this stage we have primarily considered Cartesian and polar coordinate systems. We will now show how to apply the vector techniques we have learnt to any orthogonal curvilinear coordinate system. In particular we wish to describe the vector differential operators such as grad, div and curl in curvilinear coordinates and how to change coordinates and equations between systems.

In the same way that we have generalised vector quantities from scalar quantities, we can generalise vectors via the notion of a tensor. Physical examples of tensor fields include the stress at any point in a continuum or the electromagnetic field tensor in the theory of general relativity. We will outline the different classifications of tensor and how to determine if a quantity truly is of tensor character.

At each stage of the course we will keep both mathematical rigour and physical application at the forefront, ensuring that we are both precise enough to avoid ambiguity and sufficiently motivated by any abstract concept which is applicable to the real world. The course will be largely mathematically oriented, with all theorems presented with proof (where applicable). However vector analysis was born out of physical applicability and so we will spend the remaining lectures discussing fluid dynamics and electromagnetism - two areas which will greatly benefit the development of your applied mathematics insight.
