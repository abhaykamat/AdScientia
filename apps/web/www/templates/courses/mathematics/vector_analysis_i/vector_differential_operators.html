Vector Analysis I

Vector Differential Operators

At this stage, calculus in the context of multi-dimensional functions has been discussed, including the definition of a tangent vector and the ability to integrate quantities that vary along a path through space. The notion of a scalar and vector field has been outlined including a basic discussion of their characteristics. The focus will now shift to the concept of vector differential operators and how they characterise the physical properties modelled by scalar and vector fields.

Previously it has been mentioned that certain physical fields such as a fluid velocity or gravitational force can be modelled via scalar and vector fields. Physical properties of interest might include the rate of expansion or rotation of a fluid, the strength of a gravitational force at a point, the rate of diffusion of a chemical in a solute or the heat transfer through a metallic bar. These systems may initially appear disparate, but it is possible to model them using similar mathematical tools. This chapter will discuss these tools in an abstract setting using generic scalar and vector fields, applying them to a set of physical phenomena to characterise their properties.

Two properties of interest are the homogeneity and the isotropy of the field. A homogeneous field is constant and is thus independent of the position vector argument. An isotropic field is one which appears identical when looking in all directions from a particular position vector. Note that homogeneity does not imply isotropy. Consider the planar vector field $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $x \mapsto f(x) = (1,0)$. $f$ is a constant homogeneous field but it is clearly not isotropic. To see this, consider looking along the x-axis (in a Cartesian representation) in both the positive and negative directions.

Consider a vector field modelling the velocity field of a fluid. Asking whether the fluid has any sources, sinks or whether the flow rotates at any point are natural questions. To answer them, vector differential operators will be employed. These operators are functions that take a scalar or vector field as their argument to produce another scalar or vector field. Hence they are generalisations of the familiar one-dimensional derivative which can be thought of as a map that takes a function $f\in C^1(\mathbb{R})$ and returns another function $f'\in C(\mathbb{R})$.

Four important differential operators will now be discussed: the gradient, divergence, curl and Laplacian. Each of these operators possesses a clear physical interpretation and can provide a great deal of information about the field being operated on. Later on in the course it will be shown how partial differential equations representing physical systems can be formed from combinations of these operators and fields to produce accurate models of physical systems.

Gradient

To motivate the concept of a gradient, the familiar one-dimensional derivative will be employed. This is a map that takes a function $f$ to another function $f'$ representing the best linear approximation to $f$ at a point $x$. Since $f=f(x)$ where $x \in \mathbb{R}$, it only possesses one degree of freedom, namely the variable $x$. The gradient provides a means of generalising this map to systems with additional degrees of freedom, such as scalar fields, which are maps of the form $g:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$.

Since the gradient is a generalisation of the one-dimensional derivative, it is sensible to suggest that the result of this new gradient operator will produce a vector quantity, one component for each degree of freedom present in the original field. In addition, it must also handle scalar fields of dimension one in which case it reduces to the aforementioned one-dimensional derivative.

These constraints lead to the natural definition of the most fundamental differential operator, the gradient operator $\nabla$, or grad. The gradient operator acts on a scalar field, defined on a vector space, to produce a vector field. Each point of the gradient field contains a vector of the partial derivatives of the scalar field in each dimension of the original vector space. More formally:

Definition
Consider the differentiable scalar field $f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$. Then the mapping $\nabla f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n, x \mapsto \nabla f(x) = (\partial_1 f(x), ... , \partial_n f(x))$ is called the gradient mapping and the vector $\nabla f(x) = (\partial_1 f(x), ... , \partial_n f(x))$ is called the $\textbf{gradient}$ of $f$ at the point $x$.

The most natural way to interpret the gradient field is to see that the vector at each point in the new gradient field represents the magnitude and direction of the steepest rate of change of the original scalar field. For instance, consider modelling a the temperature distribution in a large room by a scalar field. Then the direction of the gradient vector at each point represents the direction towards a warmer location, while the magnitude of the vector describes how rapidly the temperature increases in that direction.

Examples

The examples below calculate the gradient map for a group of abstract scalar fields. Each example is based on scalar fields in $\mathbb{R}^2$ or $\mathbb{R}^3$ but the calculation easily generalises to $\mathbb{R}^n$. It should be stated that at this stage, the calculation being applied is utilising a Cartesian coordinate system where the gradient has a straightforward form. In general curvilinear coordinates, it takes on a more complicated form. This will be the subject of a later chapter.

Example 1
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $(x,y) \mapsto f(x,y) = sin(x) + cos(y)$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla f(x,y) &=& \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) \\ &=& \left(cos(x), -sin(y)\right) \end{eqnarray*}
Example 2
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z) = xy + xz + yz$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla f(x,y,z) &=& \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \right) \\ &=& \left(y+z,x+z,x+y\right) \end{eqnarray*}
Example 3
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z) = (x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{-1/2} = 1/r$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla f(x,y,z) &=& \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \right) \\ &=& \left(\frac{x}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}, \frac{y}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}, \frac{z}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\right) \\ &=& r/||r||^2 \end{eqnarray*}

The last example is an instance of a potential field, which will be studied below. The representation of a potential generated by a point source is far more appropriate in a spherical polar coordinate system. It will be shown in Curvilinear Coordinates how to transform vector differential operators between differing curvilinear coordinate systems. This can sometimes dramatically reduce the difficulty in solving vector operator equations, such as partial differential equations (PDE).

The gradient operator provides a generalisation of the one-dimensional derivative map to scalar fields and is defined only for scalar fields. Vector fields by definition are vector valued and hence require a different generalisation of the notion of derivative. Intuitively, consider a change in scalar quantity between two points in a scalar field. The gradient provides us with a way to characterise this change by outlining the extent of the change in each component of the field.

A vector field provides a vector quantity at each point. An infinitesimal traversal of the field can lead to changes in both the magnitude and the direction of the field. Thus it is natural to define vector differential operators that can separately characterise the change in magnitude and direction of a vector field. These two operators are the divergence and the curl. They quantify two important local properties of physical systems: expansion and rotation.

Divergence

The $\textbf{divergence}$ operator can be thought of as determining whether there is a net inflow or outflow in an infinitesimal spatial region. Consider a compressible fluid such as a gas, the velocity of which modelled as a vector field. It is natural to ask how the gas is expanding or contracting as the field is traversed. The divergence operator acts on this vector field to produce a scalar field, each point of the latter providing us with a value about how much expansion or contraction is occuring at that point.

In Cartesian coordinates, the form of the divergence is particularly straightforward. Consider $(x_1,...x_n)$ as a system of Cartesian coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then the divergence is defined by taking the scalar product of the gradient vector (the vector of the partial derivative operators) with the vector field itself. This produces a scalar value at each point (i.e. a scalar field). A positive divergence implies a local expansion, while a negative divergence implies a contraction. A zero divergence field is known as incompressible or solenoidal. In Cartesian coordinates, the divergence is given by:

Definition
Let $\textbf{f}:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$ be a differentiable vector field. The $\textbf{divergence}$ of the vector field $f=(f_1,...f_n)$ is the scalar field $\nabla \cdot f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, x \mapsto \nabla \cdot f (x) = \sum^n_{i=1} \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_i} (x)$.

Later on it will be shown how to define the divergence in a more formal manner once the concept of flux has been defined. One must be careful at this stage - it has already been seen how vector calculus can lead to notational difficulties. As has been shown earlier on in the course, the scalar (inner) product utilised above is defined on a vector space. However, the gradient vector is not an element of this space and hence an abuse of notation occurs, by stating that the divergence is equal to $\nabla \cdot f$. To avoid this notational ambiguity, some texts write the divergence operator as $\textbf{div}$.

Examples

Example 1
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $(x,y) \mapsto f(x,y) = (x,y)$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot f(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y) = 2 > 0. \end{eqnarray*}

The field is shown to have positive divergence and hence it is expanding at every point.

Example 2
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $(x,y) \mapsto f(x,y) = (-y,x)$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot f(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-y) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x) = 0. \end{eqnarray*}

In this field, the contraction in the horizontal direction is cancelled out by the expansion in the vertical direction. Hence the total expansion at any point is zero.

Example 3
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $(x,y) \mapsto f(x,y) = (sin(y),cos(x))$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot f(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(sin(y)) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(cos(x)) = 0. \end{eqnarray*}

In this field the periodic expansion in each axis is cancelled out by the periodic contraction in the other, as in the example above. Hence the divergence of this field is zero.

Example 4
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $(x,y) \mapsto f(x,y) = (sin(x),cos(y))$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot f(x,y) &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(sin(x)) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(cos(y)) \\ &=& cos(x) - sin(y). \end{eqnarray*}

The field above is similar to the previous periodic example, with the exception that the expansion and contraction do not always cancel and can even accumulate. Since $sin(s),\;\;cos(s) \in [-1,1]$, for $s\in\mathbb{R}$ it implies that $\nabla \cdot f(x,y) \in [-2,2]$.

Curl

The divergence operator is the natural tool to discuss contraction and expansion of a vector field. Continuing with the fluid velocity field example, it can be asked whether the fluid is locally rotating at any point. The curl operator provides a means of characterising this local differential rotation.

Unlike the divergence operator, which produces a scalar field, the curl provides a vector at every point. The length and direction of this vector describes the field rotation. In order to formally define the curl, it is necessary to resort to the concept of circulation, which will be discussed in Integral Theorems. The curl is defined in such a way that it is locally orthogonal to the vector field, the direction being perpendicular to the local flow rotation plane. Unlike the divergence operator, the curl does not straightforwardly generalise to dimensions beyond three.

In Cartesian coordinates, the curl can be obtained by taking the vector (cross) product of the vector of partial derivatives and the vector field, $\textbf{f}$. Once again, this is an abuse of notation when written as $\nabla \times \textbf{f}$.

Definition: Curl ($\mathbb{R}^3$)
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$ be a differentiable vector field in space. The $\textbf{curl}$ of the vector field $f=(f_1,f_2,f_3)$ is the vector field $\nabla \times f : \mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3, (x, y, z) \mapsto \nabla \times f(x, y, z)$, given by: \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \times f = \left( {\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial f_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial z} \\ \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f_3}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y} \\ \end{array} } \right) \end{eqnarray}

To avoid the above abuse of notation, some texts write the curl as rot.

Examples

Example 1
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z) = (x,y,z)$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \times f = \left( {\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(z) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x) \\ \end{array} } \right) = \left( {\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} } \right) \end{eqnarray*}
Example 2
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z) = (-y,x,0)$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \left( {\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(0) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(0) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-y) \\ \end{array} } \right) = \left( {\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{array} } \right) \end{eqnarray*}

Curl Identities

There are two important properties of the curl which will now be addressed. The first states that the curl of any gradient of a scalar field is the zero vector field. This will have important implications for gradient vector fields which will be discussed later. The second states that the divergence of the curl of a vector field is zero. Both of the results occur because of the equality of mixed partial derivatives.

Theorem (Curl of a Gradient):
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $f \in C^2(\mathbb{R})$ be a twice differentiable scalar field. Then the curl of the gradient field of $f$ is the zero field: \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \times \nabla f = \mathbf{0} \end{eqnarray} Proof:
The gradient of $f$, $\nabla f$, is given by $\nabla f = (\partial f / \partial x, \partial f / \partial y, \partial f / \partial z)$. Hence, by the equality of mixed partial derivatives (which exist because $f \in C^2(\mathbb{R})$): \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \times \nabla f = \left( {\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial z)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial y)}{\partial z} \\ \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial x)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial z)}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial y)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (\partial f / \partial x)}{\partial y} \\ \end{array} } \right) = \left( {\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z \partial y} \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial z} \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} \\ \end{array} } \right) = \textbf{0} \end{eqnarray}
Theorem (Divergence of a Curl):
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$ be a spatial vector field. Then the divergence of the curl of the field is zero: \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times f) = 0 \end{eqnarray} Proof:
Once again the proof relies on the equality of mixed partial derivatives: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times f) &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left(\frac{\partial f_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial z}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left(\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f_3}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left(\frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y}\right) \\ &=& \left(\frac{\partial^2 f_1}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 f_1}{\partial z \partial y}\right) + \left(\frac{\partial^2 f_2}{\partial x \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 f_2}{\partial z \partial x}\right) + \left(\frac{\partial^2 f_3}{\partial y \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 f_3}{\partial x \partial y}\right) \\ &=& 0 \end{eqnarray*}

Laplacian

The final operator that will be discussed is the Laplace operator, or Laplacian, $\Delta$. The Laplacian is primarily associated with the physical concepts of smearing, diffusion and viscosity.

The net rate of diffusion of a dissolved chemical in a fluid is proportional to the Laplacian of the chemical concentration at that point. The diffusion equation is a partial differential equation which formalises this process. It will be studied in the second year course Partial Differential Equations I. The term modelling viscosity in the famous Navier-Stokes fluid equations is given by the Laplacian of the vector of scalar quantities (velocity, pressure, density). The Laplacian is an indispensible operator in the fields of gravitation, electrodynamics and fluid dynamics.

Given a scalar field $f$ the Laplacian is defined as the divergence of the gradient of the field, $\Delta f = \nabla \cdot \nabla f$. In Cartesian coordinates the Laplacian has a simple form. It is the sum of the non-mixed second-order partial derivatives of the field in each dimension. Formally:

Definition
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $f \in C^2(\mathbb{R})$ be a twice differentiable scalar field. The $\textbf{Laplacian}$ of the scalar field $f$ is the scalar field $\Delta f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $x \mapsto \Delta f(x) = \nabla \cdot \nabla f (x) = \sum^n_{i=1} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2_i}(x)$. An alternative notation for the Laplacian operator $\Delta$ is $\nabla^2$.

Examples

Example 1
Let $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z) = (x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{-1/2} = 1/r$. Then: \begin{eqnarray*} \Delta f (x,y,z) &=& \nabla \cdot \nabla f(x,y,z) = \left(\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2}\right)(x,y,z) \\ &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{-x}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(\frac{-z}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\right) \\ &=& \frac{3(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)}{(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{5/2}} - \frac{3}{(x^2 + y^2 + z^2)^{3/2}} = 0 \end{eqnarray*}

Solutions to the equation $\Delta f = 0$, known as Laplace's equation, are called harmonic functions and play an important role in modelling gravitational fields in free space. This equation will be studied extensively in Partial Differential Equations I.

Gradient Fields and Potential

If a vector field can be given as the gradient of a scalar field, then it is said to be a gradient field. Gradient fields possess numerous useful properties are another indispensible tool in physics. These fields arise naturally in certain physical situations such as particular types of gravitational or electromagnetic fields. It has already been shown that the curl of a gradient is identically zero. This implies that any gradient vector field will be irrotational.

It turns out (and will be formally proven later) that an irrotational vector field is conservative. This means that translating a particle from point to point in the field will not change its total energy. The gravitational potential energy will be translated into kinetic energy and vice versa. This is a property shared by gravitational fields and hence a gravitational force field can be written as a gradient of a scalar potential.

Definition
A vector field $f:D \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$, $D \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ is called a $\textbf{gradient vector field}$, if $\exists \Phi:D \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ with: \begin{eqnarray} f(x) = \nabla \Phi (x)\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \forall x \in D. \end{eqnarray} If such a $\Phi$ exists then it is called the $\textbf{potential}$ of $f$.

Notice that by this definition, if $\Phi$ is a potential of $f$, then so is $\Phi + k$, for $k\in\mathbb{R}$ as $\nabla (\Phi + k)(x) = \nabla \Phi(x)$. Thus the potential is only unique up to a constant. Not every vector field $f$ has a potential and thus not all are not of gradient type.

Fundamental Theorem of Vector Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Vector Calculus will now be motivated via a familiar physical scenario.

Consider a particle in the gravititational field generated by a point mass at the origin, $0$. If the particle moves from point $x$ to $y$ in the field ($x,y \neq 0)$, then due to the law of conservation of energy, the kinetic and potential energy are being exchanged as the particle's radial distance from the point source changes.

If the particle has a total energy $H$ and starts at point $x$, then regardless of how it ends up at $y$, it will still possess the same total energy, $H$. However, kinetic energy may have been exchanged for potential energy, or vice versa, while en route. This implies that the work done on the particle as it moves through the field is independent of the path taken. Any field in which this property occurs is known as a conservative field.

The notion of path-independent work will now be generalised. To achieve this, the path of the particle will be represented by a piece-wise $C^1$-differentiable curve, $\mathscr{C}$, parametrised by a map from a subset of the real line to n-dimensional Euclidean space, $\gamma:[a,b]\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$. The tangent line integral along the path $\mathscr{C}$ of the gradient of the potential field represents the work carried out on the particle as it traverses the path. It will be shown that this integral is in fact only dependent upon the values of the potential at the end points of the parametrisation of the path.

The proof of this result is very elegant. It relies on two key theorems, namely the chain rule for composite functions and the fundamental theorem of calculus in one variable:

Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Vector Calculus)
Let $\Phi:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a $C^1$-differentiable scalar field. Let $\mathscr{C} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a piece-wise $C^1$-differentiable curve parametrised by $\gamma:[a,b]\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$. Let $f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$ be a gradient vector field s.t. $\nabla \Phi = f$. Then the tangent line integral is only a funtion of the endpoints of the parameterisation and the potential: \begin{eqnarray} \int_\mathscr{C} \nabla \Phi \cdot ds = \Phi(\gamma(b)) - \Phi(\gamma(a)) \end{eqnarray} Proof:
The proof makes use of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for real valued functions and the chain rule for composite functions. If the chain rule is applied to the map $[a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $t \mapsto \Phi(\gamma(t))$, then: \begin{eqnarray} (\Phi \circ \gamma)'(t) = \nabla \Phi (\gamma(t)) \cdot \gamma'(t) \end{eqnarray} Thus by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for real valued functions: \begin{eqnarray} \int_\mathscr{C} \nabla \Phi \cdot dS &=& \int^b_a \nabla \Phi (\gamma(t)) \cdot \gamma'(t) dt \\ &=& \int^b_a (\Phi \circ \gamma)'(t) = \Phi(\gamma(b)) - \Phi (\gamma(a)) \end{eqnarray}

Vector Identities

The solution of PDE often requires manipulation of vector operators. Certain properties such as linearity provide insight into the behaviour of these equations. The following identities will certainly come in useful in later studies of theoretical or physical phenomena.

The first identities concern the linearity of the gradient function. If $f,g:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ are scalar fields, then:

  • The gradient of their sum is the sum of their respective gradients
  • The gradient of a scalar multiple of each field is the scalar multiple of the gradient of that field

This is made precise by the following:

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla(f + g) &=& \nabla f + \nabla g \\ \nabla(cf) &=& c\nabla f,\;\;\;\;c \in \mathbb{R} \end{eqnarray} Proof:
The proof of both of the above identities makes use of the linearity of the partial derivative operator, after which the results follow straightforwardly. \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla(f+g) &=& \left( \frac{\partial (f + g)}{\partial x_1}, ... \;, \frac{\partial (f + g)}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1}, ... \;, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, ... \;, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right) + \left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1}, ... \;, \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} \right) = \nabla f + \nabla g \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla(cf) &=& \left( \frac{\partial (c f)}{\partial x_1}, ...\;, \frac{\partial (c f)}{\partial x_n} \right)\\ &=& \left( c\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, ...\;, c\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right)\\ &=& c \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, ...\;, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) = c\nabla f \end{eqnarray*}

The second set of identities generalise the product and quotient rules from one dimensional calculus. If $f,g$ are as before then:

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla(fg) &=& f\nabla g + g\nabla f \\ \nabla(f/g) &=& (g\nabla f - f\nabla g)/g^2,\;\;\;\;\;\;\;g(\textbf{x}) \neq 0 \end{eqnarray} Proof:
The proofs make use of the product and quotient rule for partial derivatives, after which the results follow straightforwardly as for the linearity identities. \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla(fg) &=& \left( \frac{\partial(fg)}{\partial x_i}, ... , \frac{\partial(fg)}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& \left( f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i} + g \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}, ... , f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} + g \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& f \left( \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i}, ..., \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}\right) + g \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}, ..., \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right) \\ &=& f\nabla g + g\nabla f \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla(f/g) &=& \left( \frac{\partial(f/g)}{\partial x_i}, ... , \frac{\partial(f/g)}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& \left( \frac{1}{g^2}\left[ g \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} - f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i} \right], ... , \frac{1}{g^2}\left[ g \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} - f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} \right] \right) \\ &=& \frac{1}{g^2} \left[ g \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}, ... , \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right) - f \left( \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i}, ... , \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}\right)\right] \\ &=& (g\nabla f - f\nabla g)/g^2 \end{eqnarray*}

The third set of identities relate the behaviour of the divergence and curl over the sum of two vector fields, $u,v:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$. The divergence and curl are both linear operators and the first property of linearity is made clear by the following:

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (u + v) &=& \nabla \cdot u + \nabla \cdot v \\ \nabla \times (u + v) &=& \nabla \times u + \nabla \times v \end{eqnarray} Proof:
\begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (u+v) &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial (u_i + v_i)}{\partial x_i} \\ &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} + \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_i} \\ &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_i} = \nabla u + \nabla v \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \times (u + v) &=& \left( \frac{\partial (u_3 + v_3)}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial (u_2 + v_2)}{\partial x_3}, \frac{\partial (u_1 + v_1)}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial (u_3 + v_3)}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial (u_1 + v_1)}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial (u_2 + v_2)}{\partial x_2} \right) \\ &=& \left(\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial x_3}, \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial x_2} \right) + \\ && \left(\frac{\partial v_3}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x_3}, \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial v_3}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x_2} \right) \\ &=& \nabla \times u + \nabla \times v \end{eqnarray*}

The fourth set of identities describe the behaviour of the divergence and curl of a scalar field multiplied by a vector field. Note that if $f$ is the constant field, then $\nabla f = 0$ and hence the second term disappears in both instances. This confirms the linearity of the divergence and curl.

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (fu) &=& f \nabla \cdot u + u \cdot \nabla f \label{eqn:vector-differential-operators:divergence-scalar-vector} \\ \nabla \times (fu) &=& f \nabla \times u + \nabla f \times u \end{eqnarray} Proof:
\begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (fu) &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial (f u_i)}{\partial x_i} \\ &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}u_i + f \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} \\ &=& \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}u_i + f \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} \\ &=& u \cdot \nabla f + f \nabla \cdot u \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \times (fu) &=& \left(\frac{\partial (fu_3)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial (fu_2)}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial (fu_1)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial (fu_3)}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial (fu_2)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (fu_1)}{\partial y}\right) \\ &=& \left( f \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial y} + u_3 \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - f \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial z} - u_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}, f \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial z} + u_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - f \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x} - u_3 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, f \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial x} + u_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} - f \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial y} - u_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right) \\ &=& f \left(\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial z} \right) + \\ && \left( u_3 \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - u_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}, u_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - u_3 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, u_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} - u_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right) \\ &=& f \nabla \times u + \nabla f \times u \end{eqnarray*}

This identity concerns the divergence of a vector product. Note that if both $u$ and $v$ are irrotational, then the divergence of their vector product is zero.

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (u \times v) = v \cdot \nabla \times u - u \cdot \nabla \times v \end{eqnarray} Proof:
\begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (u \times v) &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1} (u_2 v_3 - v_2 u_3) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2} (v_1 u_3 - u_1 v_3) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_3} (u_1 v_2 - v_1 u_2) \\ &=& \frac{\partial( u_2 v_3 )} {\partial x_1} - \frac{\partial( v_2 u_3 )} {\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial( v_1 u_3 )} {\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial( u_1 v_2 )} {\partial x_2} + \frac{\partial( u_1 v_2 )} {\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial( v_1 u_2 )} {\partial x_3} \\ &=& u_2 \frac{\partial v_3 } {\partial x_1} + v_3 \frac{\partial u_2 } {\partial x_1} - v_2 \frac{\partial u_3 } {\partial x_1} - u_3 \frac{\partial v_2 } {\partial x_1} + \\ && v_1 \frac{\partial u_3 } {\partial x_2} + u_3 \frac{\partial v_1 } {\partial x_2} - u_1 \frac{\partial v_2 } {\partial x_2} - v_2 \frac{\partial u_1 } {\partial x_2} +\\ && u_1 \frac{\partial v_2 } {\partial x_3} + v_2 \frac{\partial u_1 } {\partial x_3} - v_1 \frac{\partial u_2 } {\partial x_3} - u_2 \frac{\partial u_1 } {\partial x_3}\\ &=& \left[v_1 \left(\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial x_3}\right) + v_2 \left(\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial x_1}\right) + v_3 \left(\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial x_1} - \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial x_2}\right)\right] + \\ && \left[u_1 \left(\frac{\partial v_3}{\partial x_2} - \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x_3}\right) + u_2 \left(\frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x_3} - \frac{\partial v_3}{\partial x_1}\right) + u_3 \left(\frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x_1} - \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x_2}\right)\right] \\ &=& v \cdot \nabla \times u - u \cdot \nabla \times v \end{eqnarray*}

This identity states that the Laplacian of the product of two scalar fields is, by definition, the gradient of a gradient of a product of scalar fields. It makes use of the fact that the scalar product is commutative.

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \Delta (fg) = f \Delta g + g \Delta f + 2 (\nabla f \cdot \nabla g) \end{eqnarray} Proof:
\begin{eqnarray*} \Delta (fg) &=& \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x^2_1}(fg) + \ldots + \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x^2_n}(fg) \\ &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}\left(\frac{\partial (fg)}{\partial x_1}\right) + \ldots + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n}\left(\frac{\partial (fg)}{\partial x_n}\right)\\ &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}\left( f\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} + g\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \right) + \ldots + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}\left( f\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} + g\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right)\\ &=& f \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2_1} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} + g \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2_1} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} + \ldots +\\ && f \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2_n} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} + g \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2_n} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \\ &=& f \Delta g + g \Delta f + 2 (\nabla f \cdot \nabla g) \end{eqnarray*}

This identity states that the divergence of the vector product of two gradient fields is zero. Hence the vector field orthogonal to both the gradient of $f$ and $g$ is solenoidal.

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (\nabla f \times \nabla g) = 0 \end{eqnarray} Proof: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (\nabla f \times \nabla g) &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\partial g}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right) + \\ && \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left( \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial g}{\partial z}\right) + \\ && \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) \\ &=& \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} \frac{\partial g}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x \partial z} - \\ && \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x \partial y} \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial z} + \\ && \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial y \partial x} \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial z} - \\ && \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} \frac{\partial g}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial y \partial z} + \\ && \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial z} \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial y \partial z} - \\ && \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x \partial z} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial z} = 0 \end{eqnarray*}

The final identity is a consequence of the linearity of the divergence operator and \ref{eqn:vector-differential-operators:divergence-scalar-vector}, applied to the vector field $u = \nabla g$.

Proposition:
\begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (f\nabla g - g\nabla f) = f \Delta g - g \Delta f \end{eqnarray} Proof: \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla \cdot (f\nabla g - g\nabla f) &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1} \left(f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} - g\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \right) + \ldots + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n} \left(f \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} - g\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \\ &=& \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} + f \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x_1^2} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} - g \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} + \ldots + \\ && \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n} + f \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x_n^2} - \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} - g \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \\ &=& f\left( \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2_1} + \ldots + \frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2_n}\right) - g\left(\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} + \ldots + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2_n}\right) \\ &=& f \Delta g - g \Delta f \end{eqnarray*}
